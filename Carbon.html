Carbon Sequestration

The Carbon Sequestration Potential Index (CSPI) reflects the ability of land areas to store carbon in vegetation, and is calculated based on three main variables: Gross Primary Production (GPP), Aboveground Carbon Density (ACD), and Forest Cover (FC). These indicators help identify which areas are already functioning as strong carbon sinks and which have potential for reforestation or improved land management.

  • GPP represents the amount of carbon dioxide absorbed by plants through photosynthesis. This gives us insight into how active and productive the vegetation is.
  • ACD refers to the amount of carbon stored in the biomass of trees, shrubs, and other aboveground vegetation. High ACD areas are key for conservation because they hold large carbon stocks.
  • FC indicates the percentage of land covered by forest, which supports both existing carbon storage and future sequestration capacity.

For this assignment I chose to focus on Cyprus as it is a geographically diverse island, featuring mountain ranges like the Troodos and Kyrenia, the central Mesaoria Plain, and an extensive coastline. It has a Mediterranean climate with hot, dry summers and mild, wetter winters. While the island enjoys over 300 days of sunshine a year, the Troodos Mountains receive the most rainfall, conditions that directly influence vegetation health and carbon uptake. This range in topography and climate makes Cyprus a good example to examine both existing carbon sinks and areas that could benefit from restoration.

Maps

The map was built using Google Earth Engine (GEE). I began by selecting Cyprus as my region of interest (ROI) and uploading its shapefile to the GEE platform. I then downloaded the ESA Aboveground Biomass GeoTIFF dataset that includes Cyprus, based on the islandâ€™s latitude and longitude. After importing the file into GEE and replacing the sample ROI in the script, I ran the calculations to generate ACD, GPP, FC, and CSPI layers. Cyprus covers roughly 3.6 million hectares, and the CSPI map allows us to visually compare areas of high carbon storage versus areas with potential for more vegetation growth. The values for mean GPP, ACD, FC, and CSPI were extracted directly from the console in GEE.

The conservation area I identified, found in Southwest Central Cyprus, shows high ACD, meaning these zones already store significant carbon in their forests or dense vegetation. These areas should be prioritised for protection to avoid future emissions and support biodiversity. In contrast, the reforestation area found in Northeast Central Cyprus had low ACD but moderate GPP, indicating it does not store much carbon yet but is biologically active. That makes it an ideal candidate for replanting, as it could significantly increase its carbon storage capacity over time.
