Vector Spatial Analysis

Vector spatial analysis maps are useful for showing how different features, like sidewalks, trees, or neighbourhood boundaries, relate to each other in space. Using points, lines, and polygons, they allow you to see things like how dense a feature is, how close it is to something else, or how it’s distributed across an area. This becomes really important when trying to understand things like accessibility, infrastructure quality, or where green space is lacking. These maps can reveal spatial patterns that aren’t obvious at first glance, which makes them really powerful for urban or environmental planning.

To create the final map, we used a combination of mobile data collection through ArcGIS Field Maps and spatial analysis tools within ArcGIS Online. The process began with collecting vector data in the field using mobile devices from a survey that we created as a class. Each participant contributed points by filling out a shared form that automatically geotagged observations such as sidewalk condition or the amount of bikes parked. This data was uploaded directly into ArcGIS Online, where it could be accessed and analysed in real time.

Then, using the data collected through the mobile app in ArcGIS, I attempted to analyse sidewalk conditions in relation to the population density of different neighbourhoods in Utrecht. I did this by aggregating the sidewalk condition points to the population density layer. However, the results mostly reflected the amount of data collected per area—meaning neighbourhoods with more recorded points appeared to have worse or better conditions simply because more data was submitted there. This made the analysis unreliable. If the sidewalk condition categories had been assigned numerical values (e.g., 1–3) , we could have calculated average condition scores per area, which would allow for a clearer distinction between neighbourhoods with good and poor sidewalk infrastructure. My next idea was to incorporate data on transit stops to see how they relate to areas with good sidewalk conditions, or whether they might have a negative impact on those areas. I found a dataset called World Transit Stops in the ArcGIS database and attempted to run some analysis. However, I wasn’t able to proceed because the tools I needed required more credits than I had available, one of the major drawbacks of using the online version of ArcGIS without a paid subscription.

So instead, I decided to take a different approach for the spatial analysis, something I could actually carry out. I downloaded two datasets from ArcGIS: one showing the number of trees in Utrecht, and another showing the administrative divisions between municipalities, which helped provide clearer geographic context. I used the Density tool to visualise the distribution and quantity of trees across the city. For the symbology, I chose a yellow-to-green gradient to represent tree density, as I thought it was visually fitting and thematically appropriate.

The visualisation reveals that tree coverage is densest in the central and western parts of the city, while the southern and southeastern areas show noticeably lower levels of vegetation. The distribution appears uneven, likely due to varying land use across the city, for example, higher density in residential or park areas and lower density in more built-up or industrial zones. This map is useful for identifying which neighbourhoods have access to urban green space and where additional tree planting or greening efforts could be prioritised.
