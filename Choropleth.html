Choropleth

A choropleth map is a thematic map where areas (such as countries or regions) are shaded or patterned in proportion to the value of a statistical variable being represented, such as population density, election results, or in this case, access to drinking water. Some benefits of it being that it makes complex data easily understandable through visual colour gradients which then makes it useful to identify geographic patterns and disparities. Some negatives are that it can oversimplify data, especially in large countries where regional differences are not shown, and the choice of colour scale or class intervals can distort perceptions of the map.

Maps

The map presented is a choropleth map visualising data related to the Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 6: Ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all. More specifically, it highlights Target 6.1: By 2030, achieve universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water for all. The indicator used is Indicator 6.1.1: Proportion of population using safely managed drinking water services, which measures the percentage of people in each country who had access to drinking water from an improved source that is located on premises, available when needed, and free from contamination. The data shown is from the year 2022, and countries are colored in a range of blue tones representing increasing levels of access from 0% to 100%. For full metadata and methodology on this indicator, refer to the official SDG indicator metadata page: https://unstats.un.org/sdgs/metadata/?Text=&Goal=6&Target=6.1

To create this map, I began by joining the clean global data sheet layer with the dataset for my selected indicator, SDG indicator 6.1.1. Once the layers were joined, I verified that the data matched correctly by year and country to ensure accuracy.
